Overview

The Problem

Teachers lack accessible, actionable guidance for multilingual learners.

Multilingual learners are one of the fastest-growing student demographics in the U.S. Most teachers receive inadequate training and, despite good intentions, make instructional decisions that underserve this population — oversimplifying content, over-translating materials, or missing key scaffolding opportunities entirely.

The Solution

An AI coach that recommends, explains, and builds materials on demand.

MiLLie provides research-aligned instructional strategies, explains why they work, and follows through by generating scaffolded materials, lesson adaptations, and vocabulary resources tailored to the teacher's specific context — grade level, proficiency level, and content area.

Who It's For

Early Career Teachers

Still developing their practice, with little practical training for diverse learners. Need actionable advice, ready-to-use materials, and clear reasoning — fast.

Teachers Facing New Populations

Experienced teachers encountering multilingual learners for the first time, or a sudden influx of new language backgrounds or mixed proficiency levels.

Instructional Coaches

Supporting a cohort of educators with varying needs. Need to generate exemplar materials at scale and pass along advice that works across different classrooms.

Design Decisions

Persona design

MiLLie was given a specific expert identity — multilingual learner specialist, sheltered instruction practitioner, teacher trainer. This constrains her responses to a defined scope and establishes credibility with the user. A general-purpose AI voice was deliberately avoided.

Interaction flow

The step-by-step structure mirrors gradual release of responsibility. Steps 1 and 2 gather context before any advice is given, because recommendations without grade level, proficiency level, and topic are too generic to be useful. This is a needs analysis approach applied directly to AI interaction design.

Output structure

Step 3 always produces two distinct recommendations with no overlap, grounded in pedagogy. This was a deliberate constraint to prevent repetitive or surface-level responses and push the agent toward differentiated thinking.

Generative extension

Steps 4 through 6 move the teacher from advice to application by offering to generate actual classroom materials. The goal is not information transfer but a usable product the teacher can implement the next day. This reflects task-based learning principles at the tool design level.

Guardrails

Guardrails were built in to prevent prompt leakage, off-topic responses, and collection of student information. These reflect responsible AI design practices alongside instructional ones.

Iterative build plan

The PRD structures development across four versioned releases with increasing quality targets — from 60–70% accurate generations at prototype stage to 80–90% at MVP release — measured by a composite output rating rubric and NPS targets.

Reflection

MiLLie sits at the intersection of instructional design and product thinking — which is what made it interesting to build. Writing the PRD forced me to define success before touching the prompt, and the prompt engineering work forced me to translate pedagogical principles into interaction constraints. Neither document works without the other.

The bigger insight was that AI tools for educators fail when they prioritize breadth over depth. MiLLie is narrow by design — she knows multilingual learner pedagogy well and refuses to be pulled off topic. That constraint is a feature, not a limitation.
